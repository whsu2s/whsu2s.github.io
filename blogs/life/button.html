The Culture Hidden in an Elevator

Posted on 3 July, 2025

有一次，我和朋友到德國西南部靠近瑞士的小鎮旅行，住進一家連鎖飯店。當我們辦完入住手續，搭上電梯上樓時，朋友驚訝地說：「欸？電梯竟然沒有關門鍵！」那是我在德國搭過為數不多的幾次電梯之一，這個看似微不足道的小細節，竟讓我在後來慢慢咀嚼出不少感悟。

最初我以為只是設計不同，後來查了些資料才知道，在歐美許多國家，電梯的關門鍵本來就多半只有在消防或維修模式下才有效，平常根本無法使用。某種程度上，它就像個「安慰劑按鈕」，存在是為了讓人感覺自己有主導權，實際卻可能毫無作用。 但在德國，這種「假裝給你控制權」的設計似乎並不被接受。德國人性格直接，不喜歡多餘或模糊的東西：文字怎麼拼就怎麼唸，定好的規則就怎麼執行，連日常生活中的設施設計都反映這樣的風格。他們寧可省略那顆沒實際用處的關門鍵，也不要讓人誤以為自己能操控什麼。這不是冷漠，而是一種「誠實設計」的體現。

再仔細想想，這也與他們的生活節奏有關。德國人習慣慢步調的生活。無論是等公車、等紅綠燈，還是等電梯關門，他們多半不急不躁，甚至覺得那是生活的一部分。等待對他們來說不是浪費時間，而是讓時間發生。 反觀我們的日常，電梯一打開，常會有人急著按下關門鍵。有時候並不是真的趕時間，而是我們的生活節奏已經讓我們不習慣等待。不耐煩像一種反射動作，我們總希望什麼事情都能快一點，彷彿慢下來就是一種錯誤。

那些被刻意省略的設計，看似簡單，實則蘊含著一種生活哲學。或許，在異國文化中經歷的微小衝擊，其實正是在提醒我們：不是每件事都需要我們親自操控，也不是每一次等待都意味著浪費。文化藏在飲食、語言與建築中。有時候，它也悄悄隱藏在我們每天按下、又不曾細想的那顆按鈕——或者，那顆根本不存在的按鈕裡。

During a visit to a small town near the Swiss border in southwestern Germany, a friend and I checked into a chain hotel. After completing the check-in process, we took the elevator up to our room. My friend looked around in surprise and said, “Wait—there’s no ‘close door’ button!” It was one of the few times I had taken an elevator in Germany, and that seemingly insignificant detail turned out to be something I would come to reflect on deeply over time.

At first, I thought it was just a design difference. Later, I did some reading and learned that in many Western countries, the “close door” button is often only functional in maintenance or emergency modes. Under normal use, it doesn’t do anything at all. In a way, it acts like a “placebo button” that gives people the illusion of control, even if it serves no real purpose. But in Germany, this kind of “pretend control” doesn’t seem to sit well. Germans tend to be direct by nature—they don’t like ambiguity or unnecessary features. Words are pronounced just as they’re spelled. Rules are followed as they are written. Even the design of everyday public facilities reflects this mindset. Rather than include a button that doesn’t actually work, they would rather omit it entirely—so people don’t mistakenly think they’re in control of something when they’re not. It’s not coldness. It’s what you might call a form of honest design.

Thinking more about it, this also has something to do with the pace of life. Germans are used to a slower rhythm. Whether it’s waiting for a bus, a traffic light, or an elevator door to close, they are generally calm and unhurried—many even see waiting as a natural part of life. To them, waiting isn’t a waste of time; it’s the way time unfolds. By contrast, in our everyday lives, the moment an elevator door opens, someone is often quick to press the “close” button. It’s not always because we’re in a rush, but because our fast-paced lifestyle has made us unaccustomed to waiting. Impatience becomes almost reflexive—we want things to move faster, as if slowing down is somehow a mistake.

Those deliberately omitted design choices may look simple on the surface, but they quietly carry a deeper philosophy of living. Perhaps the small cultural jolts we experience abroad are gentle reminders: that not everything in life needs to be under our control, and not every wait is a waste. People often say that culture is hidden in food, language, or architecture. But sometimes, it’s tucked away in the button we press without thinking—or in the one that simply isn’t there.
